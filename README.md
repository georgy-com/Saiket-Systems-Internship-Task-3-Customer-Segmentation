# Cutstomer-churn-segmentation-analysis, Analyzing customer churn by K means cluster and segmentation
The customer segmentation analysis provides a deep and structured understanding of how different groups of customers behave in relation to churn, and it goes beyond general trends to uncover specific high-risk and high-value segments. After cleaning the dataset and ensuring that all variables such as TotalCharges, were properly converted into numeric format, the segmentation was built on three key behavioral dimensions: customer tenure, spending level, and contract type. These dimensions are critical because they reflect how long customers stay, how much they pay, and how committed they are to the service.

The tenure-based segmentation reveals a very clear lifecycle pattern in customer behavior. Customers classified as “New Customers” (those within the first 12 months) show a churn rate of approximately 47.4%, which is extremely high and indicates that nearly half of new users leave within a short period. This suggests that the early stage of the customer journey is the most fragile and vulnerable phase. In contrast, mid-term customers (13–42 months) show a significantly reduced churn rate of about 24.9%, indicating that once customers pass the initial stage, they begin to stabilize. Long-term customers (above 42 months) exhibit the lowest churn rate of approximately 10.5%, demonstrating strong loyalty and satisfaction. This gradual decline in churn as tenure increases highlights that retention is strongly tied to how long a customer has stayed, and it emphasizes the importance of early engagement, onboarding experience, and value delivery within the first year.

When examining spending behavior, a different but equally important pattern emerges. Customers were segmented into low, medium, and high spenders based on their monthly charges. The results show that high spenders have the highest churn rate at about 35.5%, followed by medium spenders at 23.7%, while low spenders have the lowest churn rate at approximately 11.6%. This indicates that customers paying more for the service are more likely to leave, which may initially seem counterintuitive since they contribute more revenue. However, this finding suggests that higher costs may increase expectations, and if the perceived value does not match the price, dissatisfaction grows. It may also indicate that high-cost plans are not properly aligned with customer needs, leading to cancellations. On the other hand, low spenders are more stable, possibly because they perceive the service as affordable and less risky to maintain.

The contract type analysis further strengthens the understanding of churn behavior by highlighting the role of commitment. Customers on month-to-month contracts show a churn rate of 42.7%, which is significantly higher than those on one-year contracts (11.2%) and two-year contracts (2.8%). This demonstrates that flexibility, while attractive, does not promote loyalty. Customers who are not tied to long-term agreements can easily leave when they are dissatisfied or find alternatives. In contrast, long-term contracts create a form of commitment that stabilizes customer behavior and significantly reduces churn. The extremely low churn rate among two-year contract users indicates that commitment is one of the strongest predictors of retention in this dataset.

The most powerful insight from this analysis comes from combining all three segmentation dimensions—tenure, spending, and contract type—into a multi-level segmentation framework. This approach reveals micro-segments that would not be visible when analyzing each factor independently. For instance, the combination of “New Customer + High Spender + Month-to-month contract” produces an extremely high churn rate of about 69.2%, making it the most at-risk segment in the dataset. This group represents customers who are new, paying high prices, and not committed to a long-term plan, which creates a perfect condition for dissatisfaction and exit. On the other hand, the segment “Long-Term Customer + Low Spender + Two-year contract” shows almost zero churn, representing the most stable and loyal customers. These individuals are highly retained because they have stayed long enough to build trust, pay affordable prices, and are committed through long-term agreements.

The segmentation also reveals that even within similar tenure groups, behavior can differ significantly depending on spending and contract type. For example, mid-term customers with high spending and month-to-month contracts still show high churn (around 46%), indicating that tenure alone does not guarantee retention if pricing and contract conditions are unfavorable. Similarly, new customers on two-year contracts show almost no churn, demonstrating that commitment can offset the risks associated with early-stage customers.

Another critical outcome of this analysis is the identification of high-value customers at risk. By filtering customers who are classified as high spenders and have churned (excluding purely new customers), the analysis identifies approximately 1,274 customers who contribute significant revenue but are leaving the service. This is a major business concern because losing high-value customers has a direct and substantial impact on revenue. The sample data shows that many of these customers are on month-to-month contracts and often use electronic payment methods, reinforcing earlier findings about risk factors. This segment represents an urgent opportunity for targeted intervention, as retaining even a fraction of these customers could significantly improve overall profitability.

Overall, the segmentation analysis clearly demonstrates that customer churn is not random but driven by a combination of lifecycle stage, pricing sensitivity, and level of commitment. Early-stage customers, high spenders, and those on flexible contracts are consistently more likely to churn, especially when these factors occur together. In contrast, long-term customers with lower costs and strong contractual commitment are highly stable. These insights provide a strong foundation for strategic decision-making, enabling businesses to move from general retention strategies to highly targeted and data-driven interventions that address specific customer groups based on their risk level and value.
